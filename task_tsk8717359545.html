Task prompt — delivered verbatim to the model

A Tier-1 auto components manufacturer supplying brake caliper brackets to two major passenger vehicle OEMs has missed dispatch commitments in three of the last six weeks, triggering expedited freight, line-side shortage alerts from one OEM, and a potential annualized penalty exposure if service levels do not recover within the next month. The COO must decide within 72 hours whether to approve a proposed Rs 12 crore capex request for a second CNC machining cell or instead stabilize throughput through an operations-control redesign. Using only the provided files, determine the true system constraint for the brake caliper bracket line, assess whether the proposed second CNC cell should be approved, and design a Drum-Buffer-Rope operating logic for the line. Your answer must identify the correct drum resource, define what buffer should protect it, explain how upstream release should be controlled, and quantify the expected impact on throughput and WIP if the recommended action is implemented. Do not use any external sources. Solve only from the provided files. Prepare a 1-page COO decision memo with exactly these 5 sections: 1. Executive recommendation: approve or reject the second CNC cell, with a one-sentence reason. 2. True constraint diagnosis: identify the real bottleneck and explain why the apparent bottleneck is incorrect. 3. Effective capacity build: compare nominal vs effective capacity for the relevant resources. 4. DBR redesign: define the drum, buffer, and rope logic for the line. 5. Operational impact: quantify expected change in weekly throughput, WIP, and customer service risk. Key Assumptions: Demand over next 8 weeks is stable and above current output. No new labor or floor space. Only one primary action may be recommended. Use weekly throughput as main metric.

Expert Test Experienced consultants will use the the same files and would reach the same answer: reject or defer the second CNC cell, identify HT-02 as the true drum, and redesign the line using Drum-Buffer-Rope with a controlled buffer before heat treatment and upstream release subordinated to furnace-effective pace. One correct answer exists because demand is above output, no approved alternate furnace is available, immediate labor or space expansion is disallowed, and the file evidence consistently shows furnace-effective capacity and post-HT yield determine shipments

1-page DOCX COO decision memo. 5 sections:

Section 1 — Executive Recommendation: REJECT (defer) the second CNC cell. The true system constraint is the heat-treat furnace, not CNC machining. Additional CNC capacity will inflate WIP without improving shipments.

Section 2 — True Constraint Diagnosis: HT-02 is the actual bottleneck. Dashboard shows CNC at 91.2% utilization, but utilization ≠ constraint under TOC. Three hidden loss categories (downtime 17.2 hrs, restart 4.2 hrs, reload 2.0 hrs) reduce furnace time from 120 to ~96.6 hrs/wk.

Section 3 — Effective Capacity Build: Nominal furnace: 10,000/wk. After downtime: ~9,661. After 6% sharing: ~9,081. After 95.6% FPY: ~8,684 good parts/wk. CNC nominal: 10,800/wk (already exceeds system limit). Proposed post-capex CNC: 15,600/wk (irrelevant — system capped at ~8,684).

Section 4 — DBR Redesign: Drum = HT-02 (all production paced to furnace-effective throughput). Buffer = protective time buffer before heat treatment (target: 4–6 hours of WIP). Rope = upstream CNC and machining release gated by furnace consumption rate, not local utilization targets.

Section 5 — Operational Impact: Current shipments: ~8,270/wk. With DBR + HT exploitation: target 8,500–8,700/wk (recovering ~250–450 units from reduced restart loss and tighter scheduling). WIP reduction: 15–25% (eliminating pre-HT queue buildup). Customer service: dispatch reliability improves as production release matches true system pace.
